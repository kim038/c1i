North America

American Marten

COLD SNAP
The American Marten (Martes Americana) is one of the two marten species found in North America. Found primarily in Alaska, Canada, and the Pacific Northwest, the American Marten evolved to thrive in the cold and dense forests and hills of the north. Incredibly elusive, one must weather through the intense climates to witness one of these weasels.

There are several subspecies under the American Marten name, ranging across from the West to the East Coast, from the Newfoundland Marten to the Preble Marten. The American Marten is under threat of poaching and habitat loss, especially old-growth forests, which take centuries to grow and mature. However, conservation efforts help restore and protect certain parts of these regions, ensuring that there are some pieces of territory left for these elusive creatures. The American Marten is not considered endangered, and is only classified as least concern. However, precautions must still be taken, as their population numbers are still dwindling under environmetal stress.

APPEARANCE

American Martens, like the rest of the Mustelidae family, are known for its lengthy body and short legs, reaching up to 76 cm (30 in), while only reaching 15 cm (6 in) in height. They are covered with thick, brown fur, with darker legs and tail. A distinct physical trait of the American Marten, and martens in general, is its iconic yellow spot on its chest, resembling a bib. Since much of their life is spent in the trees, American Martens have semi-retractable claws that allow them to easily dig into the bark and skim the treetops.

The American Marten has a box-shaped head, with wider cheeks and ears. Its muzzle is quite narrow compared to the size of its head, and is covered with dark whiskers that help it hunt. Its fur grows significantly thicker during the winter months to help it keep warm, which is why its pelt was often used as clothing for indigenous populations during the fur trade.

Tale Of Two Variants

There are two distinct lookds for the American Marten. One looks very similar to the European Marten; mostly brown with a narrow muzzle and head. Meanwhile, the lighter variant, while sharing the same dark brown body, has a distinctly pale head, almost white, contrasting with the rest of its body, and a slightly wider muzzle and head. The lighter variant is the most well-known of the two beacuse of its unique contrast in color and its overall similarity in appearance to a domesticated house pet.

NATURE'S SNOWSHOES

The heavy snowfall of the northern regions of North America can be a hinderance for many animals, sinking them several feet. However, the Ameriacn Marten evolved to bypass the snow, growing thick fur between their paws, acting as a crude form of shoeshoes. This allows the American Marten to easily skim over the snow and essentially float over the surface.

Range

HUNTERS OF THE NORTH

American Martens are found primarily on the West Coast, more specifically the Pacific Northwest. This includes British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, Northern California, and even Montana and Idaho. These regions are populated with old-growth coniferous forests that are used as shelter and transportation throughout the year.

NORTH AMERICA'S FIERCEST PREDATOR

The American Marten is primarily carnivorous, however, they are also known to be opportunistic, eating anything edible, from small mammals, birds, eggs, fish, carrion, and fruit. Their diet is more diverse during the warmer seasons, as plants become more plentiful and nurtitionally valuable. However, during the winter months, when plans shrivel and animals migrate, the American Marten relies more on meat, becoming vicious hunters. This becomes a shock, however, for unfortunate onlookers, as it may seem like a cruel joke by nature that what appears to be a "cute" animal is equipped with knives for teeth and claws, and an appetite for the hunt.

There is quite a radical difference in the diet of the American Marten between the summer and winter months. In fact, the US Forest Services conducted a research project documenting its food sources between the seasons.

AMERICA'S TREE HUNTERS

American Martens spend much of their time in the trees, navigating through the treetops in rapid strides and zigzags. However, they are also mobile on land and in water, though they avoid to demonstrate such skills when they can. Since majority of their lives are spent above the ground and in the trees, they've adapted to become one of the most experienced and skilled tree hunters and climbers in North America, rivalring their own prey, the squirrel. Since American Martens don't hibernate during the winter, their bodies adapt to the changing temperatures and climates, growing winter fur that helps them remain warm and also assist them during hunts.

Despite their bold hunting patterns, American Martens are incredibly reclusive and shy with each other, and to humans, only interacting with other martens during combat and mating season. They are rarely spotted by people, hardly venturing out of their territory.

Important Trees

Shore Pine Tree

The Shore Pine Tree (Pinus Contorta) is one of the many coniferous tree species in the Pacific Northwest that the American Marten uses to survvive. Also known as the Lodgepole Pine, the tree is mostly found along the coast in North America, and is rather small compared to its cousins, growing up to only 9 m (30 ft) tall. However, even with thsi rather small size, it offers brief, but useful shelter from predators, and a bountiful source of prey.

Douglas Fir Tree

The Douglas Fir Tree (Pseudotsuga Menziesii) are one of the most valuable trees in North America, making up much of its old-growth forest populations. Growing up to 76 m (250 ft) tall, the Douglas Fir Tree is integral to the survival of the American Marten. Both the Coast Douglas Fir and the Rocky Mountain Douglas Fir are used by the American Marten. Its tall height allows the martens to scope out the forest and protect them from predators, such as eagles, wolves, and bears. Its thick trunk also offers useful shelter for the American Marten, acting as its den.
